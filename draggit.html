00 The Goal

Fix the friction. Ship it publicly.

LinkedIn's "Add Note" modal opens directly over the profile you're trying to reference — hiding the name, title, and company at the exact moment you need them. The goal wasn't just to solve it for myself. It was to build something lightweight enough to be technically feasible, fast enough to ship with AI tools, and safe enough to be used publicly — with zero personal data being parsed or recorded.

Fix the UX friction

Make it possible to reference the profile while writing the connection note — without closing and reopening the modal.

Ship it publicly

Get it live on the Chrome Web Store — not just a prototype. A real product that real users can install.

Zero personal data

Any solution had to work without scraping or recording the profile being viewed. Privacy-safe by design.

01 Problem

The modal that blocked your context.

LinkedIn's "Add Note" modal opens directly on top of the person's profile, hiding their name, title, and company at the exact moment you need it to write something personal. So you close the modal, re-read the profile, reopen the modal, try to hold the details in working memory, and start writing. Every time. For every connection.

A small friction, but one that compounds fast when you're actively networking. And for a platform built around meaningful connection, it was a surprisingly avoidable problem.

02 Design Process

Two decisions. Both tested.

I started by sketching on paper — not Figma. Six approaches for the same constraint: how do you expose profile context without creating a new interaction pattern? Each one tested against three questions: does it feel native? Does it break LinkedIn's layout? Can it be built safely without reading personal data?

One concept cleared all three. Then the icon needed two tries.

Why this concept won
The drag handle was the only answer that met all three constraints.
01
Lightweight & feasible

A drag handle is a single injected DOM element. No new UI surface, no new modal state, no layout interference.

02
Buildable with AI

Drag logic is a well-understood pattern. Clear enough to PRD, clear enough for Cursor to build correctly — no ambiguous edge cases to hallucinate around.

03
Zero personal data

The extension only moves the modal. It doesn't read, scrape, or record anything about the person you're connecting with. Privacy-safe from day one.

03 Build Process

Vibecoded with AI tools.

I didn't just prompt-and-paste. I treated AI as a specialized collaborator on each phase of the build, with a different tool playing a different role. The design and product decisions stayed mine throughout.

🤖
ChatGPT

Before writing code, used ChatGPT to evaluate technical feasibility of all six solution options I'd designed in Figma. Key question: what can a Chrome extension actually do to LinkedIn's DOM? Answer confirmed that injecting a drag handle via content script was viable.

✳️
Claude

Wrote the full PRD, translating interaction specs, edge cases, and design decisions into structured engineering requirements. Having a PRD meant Cursor had clear context and constraints to work within, not just vibes.

⬛
Cursor

Built the Chrome extension: manifest, content script, drag logic, position memory. I directed the build, reviewed every output, debugged failures, and iterated when LinkedIn's dynamic DOM caused issues. Cursor wrote code; I owned the product.

💠
Figma Make

After the extension worked functionally, used Figma Make to rapidly explore UI variations of the drag handle, color, size, shape, and hover states without touching code each time. Landed on the purple gradient circle that feels distinct from LinkedIn's blue.

04 The Product

One drag. Full context restored.

Draggit adds a single drag handle to LinkedIn's "Add Note" modal. That's it. No settings, no onboarding, no permissions beyond what's needed.

01
Drag to reveal

Move the modal out of the way to clearly see the name, title, and company you're writing to — without closing anything.

02
No more close-and-reopen

Reference the profile while you're writing. No need to close the modal, re-read, and reopen just to double-check a spelling.

03
More confident networking

Write a personalised note knowing you have the full context in front of you. Fewer generic notes. More genuine first impressions.

07 Reflection

What shipping taught me.

Community > Research sprints

A single LinkedIn post replaced weeks of formal user research. Real people told me what interviews would have taken much longer to surface.

PRD first, even with AI

Clear requirements before touching Cursor meant fewer misaligned outputs. Every time something went wrong, it traced back to something I hadn't specified.

Rejection = the best brief

The Chrome Store rejection forced me to actually understand the permissions model. I carry that knowledge into every future build. It was the most educational moment of the whole project.

AI executes. I decide.

I used AI to accelerate execution, never to make product decisions. Direction, UX calls, iteration judgment — those stayed mine throughout.
