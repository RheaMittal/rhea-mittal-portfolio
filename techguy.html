00 Problem

Three user types. One fragmented experience.

TechGuy's operations ran entirely through WhatsApp. Customers messaged to book repairs. Engineers received work orders via text. Operations coordinated 15+ conversations across customer and engineer group chats simultaneously.

The result: lost messages, no status visibility, no structured workflow. And the deeper problem: nobody thought it was a problem.

Lost messages in busy group chats No clear status visibility Manually searching for contacts Information scattered across conversations No structured workflow
📱 Customer

Booking repairs through informal WhatsApp messages. No visibility into repair status. No way to verify TechGuy's credentials. Handing over a high-value device to a contact they can't verify.

🔧 Engineer

Receiving work orders via text in mixed personal and work group chats. Spending 3-4 minutes per job scrolling to find the right customer context before being able to act.

🖥️ Operations

Juggling 15+ fragmented conversations simultaneously. No workload visibility, no data, no way to prioritize urgent vs routine requests. I designed this dashboard end-to-end.

01 Research

What they said vs what we observed.

Before designing anything, I conducted user interviews and usability testing with 10+ users across all three user types. The initial findings were surprising. Then the observational study changed everything.

What they told us
Initial interviews
  • "WhatsApp works fine, just some minor inconveniences"
  • "I can find what I need eventually"
  • "The current system isn't really a problem"
What we observed
Observational study
  • Admins spent 3-5 minutes searching chat history for a single update
  • Engineers made audible frustrated sounds while typing contact names in search
  • Customers messaged the wrong number 40% of the time due to misspelled names
The turning point: People adapt to broken systems without realizing it. In initial interviews, everyone said WhatsApp was fine. The moment I watched an admin spend 4 minutes scrolling to find one customer update, the story changed completely. Observational research reveals what interviews cannot.
02 Users

Three users. Three completely different unmet needs.

The same platform, three completely different mental models and failure points. Designing for one without understanding the others would have produced something that technically shipped but solved nothing.

Customer
"My friend told me about TechGuy, but when I contacted them on WhatsApp, it felt too casual. I'm handing over my ₹60,000 MacBook. I need to know this is legitimate."
  • No way to verify TechGuy's authorization credentials
  • Couldn't track repair status, causing constant anxiety about device safety
  • Messages lost in personal chat history
  • No history to reference past repairs or warranties
Engineer
"During initial interviews, engineers insisted WhatsApp was easy to manage. Then I shadowed them and asked them to find a specific customer's status."
  • 3-4 min per job scrolling mixed personal and work chats
  • No clarity on job status (pending / waiting for parts / done)
  • Parts information buried across multiple WhatsApp threads
  • No structured way to update customers on progress
Operations
"Right now we're managing everything on WhatsApp with customers and engineers. It works, but things get missed and there's no real way to track or stay organized."
  • Manually tracking jobs across 15+ conversations
  • No visibility into engineer workload for smart assignments
  • No data to analyze patterns, peak times, or bottlenecks
  • Can't prioritize urgent vs routine requests at a glance
03 Design Direction

From WhatsApp chaos to a connected ecosystem.

How might we
Build a professional repair platform that creates trust in a market where 88% of people rely on word-of-mouth recommendations?

I translated user insights into clear design principles for each interface. Before touching any screens, I mapped the complete service flow, every decision point and information handoff between users, to ensure all three interfaces would function as one connected ecosystem rather than three isolated tools.

Customer
Build trust through professional brand and constant visibility

In a market where 88% trust personal recommendations, the interface itself had to feel as trustworthy as one. Testimonials front and center. Repair status always visible without asking.

Engineer
Replace fragmented chats with visual job management

Three-tab dashboard: Ongoing, Completed, Needs Parts. Every job at a glance, no scrolling. The 3-4 minute WhatsApp search problem becomes a 3-second tap.

Operations
Create a centralized command center with workload visibility

Real-time view of each engineer's active request count. Answer "Who should I assign this to?" instantly. Instant filters for urgent vs routine. No more scrolling 15 conversations.

04 Final Designs

Three connected interfaces. One service ecosystem.

Before shipping high-fidelity designs, I tested low-fidelity prototypes with real users to catch critical gaps early. Key iterations: engineers needed part status visible directly on job cards (not buried in menus); customers wanted quick access to request history; operations needed instant filters for urgent requests. These directly shaped the final interfaces.

The 20% reduction in task completion time was grounded in direct observation. Engineers who spent 3-4 minutes per job scrolling through WhatsApp completed the same task in under a minute with the three-tab interface. The structure answered the question before they had to ask it.

The 30% faster design-to-dev cycles came from the shared design system and annotated handoffs built across the team. Engineers weren't guessing at edge cases. The documentation answered questions before they were asked.

06 Reflection

What this project taught me about design.

Leading a team across three interconnected products changes how you think. Every design decision had to hold up across three different user contexts and function as one ecosystem when they came together.

People adapt to broken systems without realizing it. The "WhatsApp is fine" moment in initial interviews was the most important lesson of this project. Observational research reveals what interviews cannot: friction users have normalized is still friction, and it's still the design problem to solve.
Systems thinking is the highest-leverage skill in a multi-product context. Designing for one user in isolation would have failed. The value came from understanding how all three interfaces connected: a job created in the customer portal had to be immediately visible to the engineer and operations simultaneously.
Strategic decisions in ambiguity require holding competing priorities without rushing. Working in a startup with evolving requirements meant balancing user needs, business objectives, and technical constraints simultaneously, and making fast decisions while maintaining design quality.
Data is how designers advocate for users. When I presented the visual dashboard concept that contradicted the "WhatsApp is fine" feedback, the observational data was what made the case. Designers who can show what they observed, not just what they designed, are far more effective at influencing product direction.
