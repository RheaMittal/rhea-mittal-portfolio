00 Problem

Three user types.
Three mental models.
One brief.

TechGuy connects customers with technicians for at-home electronics repairs: laptops, printers, IT services. Three very different people needed to use this platform simultaneously: a customer booking a job, a technician showing up to fix it, and an operations team coordinating everything in between.

The brief gave us the products but not the constraints. No shared design language, no component library, no established process for a 4-person team working in parallel. The first decision wasn't a design decision; it was a systems decision.

The real risk: Three designers working independently would produce three inconsistent products. We needed a shared foundation before anyone opened a new frame.
📱 Customer App

People booking at-home electronics repairs. Mobile-first, low context. They want to book a job and track it: fast, simple, no friction. Task completion over feature depth.

🔧 Technician App

Field workers showing up to fix things. Job-focused, on the move. They need to see their schedule, job details, and status updates without stopping to think. Speed and clarity are everything.

🖥️ Operations Dashboard

Internal team managing job allocation, tracking, and platform health. High context, data-dense. I personally designed this end-to-end: it's where the most complexity lives.

Customer
"Just make it work."

They don't read instructions. They tap, get confused, tap again. The interface had to be self-evident at every step. Anything requiring memory was a failure.

Technician
"Tell me what I need, nothing more."

Field workers are in transit and under time pressure. They need their next job, location, and details immediately. Anything buried in a menu is a problem they'll ignore.

Operations
"I need it fast."

The ops team was managing jobs through WhatsApp, scrolling for 4 minutes just to get context on a single job. Efficiency wasn't a nice-to-have. It was the whole problem.

02 The Key Decision

Build the system
before building
the products.

The obvious move was to start designing immediately: one designer per product, ship fast. I pushed back. Three designers working independently would produce three inconsistent interfaces with no shared logic. I proposed spending the first week building a shared component library before any of us opened a product frame.

03 How I Led the Team

I didn't just design.
I built the process
that made it ship.

Leading a 4-person design team meant doing two jobs: I personally designed the Operations Dashboard end-to-end while setting the conditions for the rest of the team to own their surfaces. I also ran research, usability testing, and feedback cycles across all three products; the process was mine to define, not just the output.

The structure I built (daily standups, bi-weekly critiques, a shared decision log, and annotated handoffs) cut design-to-engineering cycles by 30%. Not by moving faster, but by eliminating the back-and-forth that slows every team down.

📅
Daily Standups
15 minutes. Three questions: What shipped? What's blocked? What decision needs a call today? Kept alignment without killing design flow.
👁
Bi-Weekly Critique
All three product surfaces reviewed together every two weeks. Inconsistency surfaced here, before engineering ever saw it.
📝
Decision Log
Shared Notion doc tracking every design decision and its rationale. No repeated debates. Gave engineering a source of truth during handoff.
🤝
Annotated Handoffs
Every spec included interaction notes, edge cases, and component references. Engineers stopped chasing designers for clarifications.

06 Reflection

What leading
a team taught me
about design.

Running a team changes how you design. Every choice has to be legible, defensible, and replicable by three other people. That constraint made me a sharper designer, not a slower one.

Systems thinking is the highest-leverage skill in a team context. The week we spent building a shared component library before touching any product frame saved us four times that in revision cycles. Upfront cost was real. Downstream savings were compounding.
Different users need genuinely different things, not reskinned versions of the same solution. Consumer, operator, and enterprise clients had fundamentally different relationships with the product. Holding three mental models simultaneously is harder than it sounds, and worth it.
The decision log was the most underrated artifact we made. Every design decision written down with its rationale meant we never relitigated the same debate twice. It gave engineers something to read instead of someone to chase: the real reason handoffs got faster.
Leading means explaining your reasoning, not just your outcomes. I couldn't just show the team a design that "felt right." I had to articulate why: the user insight, the alternatives, the constraint that ruled them out. That discipline made my own design thinking sharper.
